i.

What happened on June twentieth

I went to an indoor shooting range last Saturday. I shot for over an hour with my new suppressor, which is something that took about a month to get.

Suppressors are wonderful for hearing protection, but here's the thing I didn't fully appreciate. They hold the burning gas inside the gun longer than an unsuppressed rifle does. That gas, the byproducts of gunpowder combustion, comes back toward the shooter's face rather than going downrange. Outdoors, no problem. The breeze takes it away.

Indoors, that gas builds up in the air around you. Even with the good ventilation the range advertises, an enclosed space can't clear it the way open air does. Especially over the course of a long suppressed session.

I came home with a real headache and what felt like brain fog. I assumed I just needed water and sleep. By Sunday I felt much better. By Monday I was around eighty percent. But there's been a low-grade cloudiness hanging around longer than expected.

ii.

What I was actually breathing

Three things, all of which build up indoors:

  • Carbon monoxide, the same gas that comes from car exhaust. A natural byproduct of gunpowder burning. It binds to your red blood cells, blocking them from carrying oxygen properly. Mild exposure causes exactly what I felt: headache, lightheadedness, mental fog.
  • Lead, since the primers that ignite the gunpowder contain lead, which vaporizes and becomes airborne particles. Lead is a neurotoxin, and it accumulates in the body over time.
  • Other combustion byproducts like nitrogen oxides, unburned powder, various other chemicals. None of it kind to the lungs or the nervous system.

An hour of breathing this in a contained space is genuinely harmful exposure. Not you'll die levels, but enough to cause real symptoms and slow the recovery. Most experienced suppressor shooters have a similar wake-up moment early in their journey. I now know to keep suppressed shooting outdoors going forward.

The line above shows roughly how I've felt each day. Saturday was the worst. Headache all day, real fog, just feeling unlike myself. Each day since has been better, but slower than I'd hoped. Today I'd put myself at maybe 80 to 90 percent. Still some heaviness in my head, some occasional mental cloudiness.

This pattern matches what doctors call persistent post-CO cognitive symptoms. In plain terms, the brain cells that got starved of oxygen during the exposure can take days or even weeks to fully bounce back, even after the carbon monoxide itself has cleared from the body. The "fog" feeling is those cells slowly getting back to normal function. Most people fully recover from mild exposure within one or two weeks. Some take a little longer. There's a treatment that can speed things along, which is where the next part comes in.

iv.

The Buddha Bella mistake

You may remember I mentioned doing a hyperbaric session this morning. I bought a Groupon for $36 at Buddha Bella Healing Center, thinking it would help with the lingering fog.

Here's what I didn't know going in. There are two very different kinds of hyperbaric chambers, and they do very different things.

what I need
Chamber Hard-shell, FDA-approved medical

The Vitaeris is to medical hyperbaric oxygen what a foot massage is to physical therapy. Both feel lovely. Only one treats what I have.

To be fair, mild hyperbaric chambers aren't useless. Athletes use them, wellness folks swear by them. They have some real effects. They're simply not strong enough to treat what carbon monoxide does to the body. Buddha Bella isn't running anything dishonest. They're a wellness clinic with wellness-grade equipment, and I bought a Groupon without knowing the difference.

I came out of the hour-long session feeling about the same as when I went in. Some ear pressure during compression and decompression. No noticeable improvement in the cloudiness. Lesson learned, $36 worth of education about why pressure ratings matter.
